Category Theory Summer Course 2019

by David Forsman

  • First week
    • Definition of a category
    • Initial and terminal objects
    • Morphisms and some special morphisms: isomorphisms, retractions, sections, epimorphisms and monomorphisms.
    • New categories from old ones: subcategories, a dual (opposite) category and dual statements
  • Second week
    • Monday: Duality in category theory. We defined products and coproducts.
    • Tuesday: We defined functors and looked at examples: objects, morphisms, hom-functor, homotopy, homology, forgetful and free. We defined the category of arrows, slice category and coslice category. We ended the lecture with natural transformation between functors.
    • Additional lecture on Wednesday: After sum and product, there's exponentiation. We defined cartesian closed category and looked at examples. Application of Yoneda lemma was seen.

Description of the course

Approximately one year ago a group of enthusiastic mathematicians started the Category Theory Reading Group which has been active since then. Because reading category theory independently is a challenging task and there is a growing interest in the topic, this year there will be a lectured course about category theory.

Category theory is a field of mathematics in which a language is created to look for very general patterns that permeate through all of mathematics. Category theory gives a new, very algebraic lense to view mathematics. Itâ€™s a lot of fun but it requires that one has studied the object which we put into the categories.

The topics are the same as in Introduction to Category Theory I, but made a bit easier. The outline of the course is roughly following:

  • Definition of category
  • Category theoretical constructions
  • Functors and natural transformations
  • If there is time, either limits in category theory or Yoneda's lemma

Prerequisites

It would be necessary to know basic set theory and especially intersections and unions over different (possibly uncountable infinite) indexing sets. The ideas from abstract algebra, such as groups, subgroups, quotient groups, homomorphisms and equivalence relations will come up. It would be nice if people know that a continuous function can be defined as a function such that the inverse image of any open set is an open set.

Either way, everybody who is interested in the topic is welcome to join our study group or just to visit the lectures!
